# Shmuplations: The History of Sega Console Hardware

**Source:** https://shmuplations.com/segahistory/
**Original Publication:** Famitsu DC, November 1998
**Format:** Translated retrospective narrated by Hideki Sato
**Date Accessed:** 2026-02-15

---

## Overview

This is a translated interview/retrospective originally published in Famitsu DC magazine in November 1998, timed to the Dreamcast launch. The primary narrator is **Hideki Sato**, described as a longtime Sega company man who directed R&D during the 16-bit era and later became Sega president in 2002. Sato walks through the entire history of Sega's home console hardware from the SG-1000 through the Dreamcast, providing firsthand technical and business insights.

This is an exceptionally valuable primary source for the biography as it is Sato himself narrating the history.

## Hideki Sato: Direct Quotes and References

### On the Dreamcast's Identity
> "If I had to sum up succinctly what makes the Dreamcast special, I would say it's connectivity."

### On Sega's Fanbase
> "I've been told there are many Sega fans in Japan alone...though I sometimes wonder if they aren't just rooting for the underdog."

### On Sega's Legacy
> "The Dreamcast represents a recapitulation for us of 20 years of determination."

### On the Mega Drive's Design
> "We painted the body black and put the '16BIT' lettering in a gold print. That gold printing, by the way, was very expensive."

### On Third-Party Development for Genesis/Mega Drive
> "I remember, at that time, seeing the first third-party developed game, Thunder Force II, and being surprised at how well it took advantage."

### On the Mega Modem
> "We made very little money off the Mega Modem, so even at Sega, hardly anyone understood it."

### On the Saturn's Difficulties
> "The software development libraries were also insufficient, so third parties saw the Saturn as a difficult system."

### On the Significance of the Modem/Connectivity
> "With graphics and sounds, if you don't increase the power by magnitude of x100, users won't notice...The modem represents that new direction."

### On SC-3000 Advertising
> "In our commercials we used the comic duo 'Tunnels' to advertise the console—before it was even available on-sale!"

### Chip Procurement for Genesis/Mega Drive
Sato recounts negotiations with Signetics (a Motorola licensee) for 300,000 Motorola 68000 chips at unreasonably low prices. He describes the triumphant moment: "We got it!!!" when the blanket order was confirmed. This anecdote reveals Sato's hands-on involvement in chip procurement and cost negotiations — a critical aspect of hardware R&D leadership.

- **Longtime Sega company man** who directed R&D during the 16-bit era
- Later became **President of Sega** in 2002

## Console Development History (as narrated by Sato)

### SG-1000 (1983)
- Sega's first home console, following arcade success
- **CPU:** uPD780-1 (MSX Z80A compatible)
- **Graphics:** TMS-9918
- **Media:** Cartridge
- **Sales:** 160,000 units in first year (exceeded internal estimate of 50,000 by 3x)
- Evolved from the SC-3000, a beginner computer with keyboard
- Design was later self-criticized as "really horrible" — cartridges described as looking "like a tombstone"

### SG-1000 II
- Redesigned exterior for improved aesthetics
- Same internal hardware as SG-1000

### Sega Mark III
- **Key development:** New graphics chip created in-house to replace the TMS-9918
- Built specifically to compete with the Nintendo Famicom
- Response to Famicom's library advantage built through third-party relationships

### Master System
- Mark III adapted for the American market
- Particularly successful in Europe
- Released one year after the NES, resulting in approximately 10% domestic market share vs. Nintendo's dominance

### Mega Drive / Genesis (1988)
- **CPU:** Motorola 68000 (16-bit)
- **Architecture basis:** Arcade System 16 board technology
- **Audio:** FM sound
- **Design:** Black body with gold "16BIT" lettering (expensive gold printing)
- **Launch title:** Altered Beast
- **Genesis in America:** Over 15 million units sold
- **Japan:** Overshadowed by Super Famicom
- **Chip procurement:** Sato personally negotiated with Signetics for 300,000 68000 chips at aggressive pricing

### Mega Drive Peripherals
- **Mega CD:** Attempted to extend Mega Drive lifecycle
- **Mega Modem:** 1200 bps rate; used for competitive baseball and mahjong games
  - Provided key insights into user networking behavior: approximately 50/50 split between competitive gaming and email usage
  - Sato notes they made very little money from it, and few at Sega understood its significance
- **Mega Adapter:** Additional peripheral
- **XBAND service:** Later, on Saturn, reached approximately 15,000 users

### TeraDrive
- Co-produced with IBM
- First multimedia PC/Mega Drive hybrid
- Suffered from poor marketing — Sato notes the word "multimedia" didn't even exist yet
- Ahead of its time conceptually

### Sega AI Computer
- Educational hardware
- Featured a cassette tape deck (later abandoned due to poor loading times)
- Hybrid approach: voice playback via cassette, programs loaded via My Cards
- **Sales:** Approximately 5,000 units (mail-order/direct sales only)

### Sega Pico
- **Graphics chip and CPU** from the Mega Drive
- **Tablet** with positional sensor technology derived from 1987 arcade medal game "World Derby"
- Described as a "Mega Drive + tablet + voice synthesis device" hybrid

### Saturn (1994)
- **CPU:** Hitachi SH-2
- **Format debate:** Originally debated between cartridge (code-named "Jupiter") and CD-ROM; CD-ROM won due to storage capacity advantages
- **Architecture:** Dual engine approach — both sprite-based AND 3D CG capability
- **Media:** CD-ROM; GD-ROM media development led by Yu Suzuki's 3D CG research
- **Sales:** 5 million units in Japan; struggled significantly overseas
- **Key challenge:** Difficult development environment for third parties; insufficient software development libraries

### Dreamcast (1998)
- **CPU:** Hitachi SH-4 (evolution from Saturn's SH-2)
- **Graphics:** NEC Power VR2 chip — chosen over 3dfx after long internal debates
  - Described as "100 times more powerful than Saturn"
  - Power VR2 selected as next-generation approach vs. more orthodox 3dfx
- **Media:** GD-ROM (double the storage capacity of CD-ROM)
  - DVD rejected due to high costs and authoring complexity at the time
- **Key differentiator:** Standard integrated modem
- **Development partnerships:** Microsoft (Windows CE), NEC
- **Design philosophy:** "Play and communication" keyword

## Sega's Arcade-to-Home Strategy

A consistent thread throughout the article is Sega's pipeline from arcade to home:

- **System I / System II** (8-bit arcade boards) informed SG-1000 and Mark III development
- **System 16** (16-bit arcade board) directly informed the Mega Drive/Genesis architecture and the choice of the 68000 CPU
- **Yu Suzuki's 3D CG research** in arcade contexts became foundational for Saturn and Dreamcast development
- Sato describes an internal attitude: "we'll just port our arcade games" — which was both a strength (proven technology) and weakness (weak third-party relationships)

---

## Corporate Culture and Internal Dynamics

### Third-Party Relations
- Early Sega had weak third-party developer relationships compared to Nintendo
- Internal attitude was heavily focused on porting arcade titles rather than cultivating external developers
- By the Saturn era, this became a significant problem: insufficient development libraries made the Saturn "a difficult system" for third parties
- Thunder Force II (by Technosoft) is cited by Sato as a memorable early third-party title that surprised him with its quality

### Innovation Philosophy
Sato highlights several "ahead of their time" innovations:
1. **Sega My Card** — 2mm thick game format, described as ahead of the smart card market by approximately 10 years
2. **Modem networking** — The Dreamcast's built-in modem was Sega's third attempt (after Mega Modem and Saturn XBAND)
3. **Power VR2 selection** — Choosing next-generation graphics architecture over the safer 3dfx option

### User Research Insights
- Saturn modem XBAND data showed a 50/50 split between competitive gaming and email usage — informing Dreamcast's "connectivity" focus
- **Print Club example:** Sato cites high school students creating unexpected play patterns with Print Club kiosks as a model for open-ended, user-created gameplay. His takeaway: developers should enable creative spaces rather than dictate how users play.

## Competition and Market Context

- **Nintendo Famicom:** Held a virtual monopoly in Japan; its third-party ecosystem was far more developed than Sega's
- **NES in America:** Released before the Master System, giving Nintendo first-mover advantage
- **Super Famicom:** Dominated the Japanese 16-bit market, overshadowing the Mega Drive domestically
- **Genesis in America:** More successful than in Japan with over 15 million units sold, suggesting stronger Western market resonance

## Significance for Biography

This source is **critically important** as a primary source — it is Hideki Sato himself narrating the full arc of Sega's hardware history. Key biographical elements:

1. **Sato's hands-on technical involvement:** The chip procurement anecdote (negotiating with Signetics for 68000 chips) shows him directly involved in hardware engineering decisions, not just managing from above.

2. **His philosophical evolution:** From "just port arcade games" to the Dreamcast's "play and communication" vision shows significant growth in his thinking about what game hardware should be.

3. **His self-awareness:** The "rooting for the underdog" comment suggests awareness of Sega's market position and a certain wry humor about it.

4. **His long-term vision:** The repeated emphasis on connectivity — from Mega Modem through XBAND to Dreamcast's built-in modem — suggests Sato was a consistent advocate for networked gaming years before it became standard.

5. **His candor about failures:** Acknowledging the Saturn's difficult development environment and the Mega Modem's poor financial performance shows a willingness to learn from mistakes.

6. **Timeline confirmation:** Places Sato as R&D director during the 16-bit era (late 1980s–early 1990s) and confirms his trajectory to the presidency in 2002.
