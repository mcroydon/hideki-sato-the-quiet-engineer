## Detailed Findings

### Technical Specifications of the Master System / Mark III

**Claim #1: TMS9918A had "sixteen fixed colors"**
- VERIFIED. The TMS9918A has a fixed palette of 15 displayed colors plus one transparent color (color 0), commonly described as 16 colors. Sources: TMS9918 Wikipedia; MSX Wiki; TMS9918A data manual.

**Claim #2: Custom VDP Sega 315-5124 provided "thirty-two simultaneous colors from a palette of sixty-four"**
- VERIFIED. The 315-5124 supports 32 simultaneous on-screen colors (two 16-color palettes: one for background tiles, one for sprites) from a total palette of 64 colors. Sources: Copetti Master System Architecture; SMS Power; Sega Retro.

**Claim #3: "sixty-four sprites, eight per scanline, in sizes of either 8x8 or 8x16 pixels"**
- VERIFIED. The VDP supports up to 64 sprites per frame, limited to 8 per scanline, in sizes of 8x8 or 8x16 pixels. Sources: Copetti Master System Architecture; SMS Power Development documentation.

**Claim #4: "extended mode at 256x224"**
- VERIFIED. The Mark III/Master System VDP added a 256x224 resolution mode in addition to the standard 256x192 inherited from the SG-1000. Sources: Copetti Master System Architecture; SMS Power Development/Modes documentation. Note: A 256x240 mode was also added for PAL regions.

**Claim #5: Mark III VDP based on "System 2 arcade board"**
- VERIFIED. Multiple sources confirm the Mark III's custom VDP was derived from Sega's System 2 arcade board graphics hardware. Sources: Nicole Express "I Am the Mark III" (2021); Shmuplations interview; Wikipedia Master System article; research files.

**Claim #6: 8 KB main RAM (eightfold increase from 1 KB)**
- VERIFIED. The SG-1000 had 1 KB main RAM; the Mark III/Master System had 8 KB -- an eightfold increase. Sources: Copetti; Wikipedia Master System; Wikipedia SG-1000.

**Claim #7: Yamaha YM2413 OPLL FM synthesis: "nine channels"**
- VERIFIED. The YM2413 provides nine concurrent FM synthesis channels (or six melody + five rhythm in percussion mode). Sources: Yamaha YM2413 Wikipedia; SMS Power YM2413 documentation.

**Claim #8: "Mega Cartridges that could hold up to 4 Mbit"**
- VERIFIED. The Mark III/Master System supported Mega Cartridges with up to 4 Mbit (512 KB) capacity. Sources: Wikipedia Master System; research files.

**Claim #9: Sega Card format: "Only two millimeters thick"**
- VERIFIED (via primary source). The 2mm thickness claim comes from Sato's own 1998 Famitsu DC interview translated by Shmuplations. While independent physical measurement sources are scarce, the primary source (Sato himself) is authoritative. The research files also corroborate this. Sources: Shmuplations interview; research files.

### Launch Dates and Prices

**Claim #10: Mark III launched "October 20, 1985" at "15,000 yen"**
- VERIFIED. Sources: Wikipedia Master System; Sega Wiki; research files all confirm October 20, 1985, at 15,000 yen.

**Claim #11: Master System NA launched "September 1986" at "$150 to $200"**
- VERIFIED. The Master System launched in North America in September 1986 with a retail price between $150 and $200. Sources: Wikipedia Master System; research files.

**Claim #12: Master System Japan launched "October 1987"**
- VERIFIED. The chapter says "October 1987" without specifying the exact date. Research files indicate October 18, 1987, at 16,800 yen. The chapter's "October 1987" and "16,800 yen" are both correct.

**Claim #13: Europe launched "August 1987"**
- VERIFIED. The Master System launched in Europe in August 1987 (some sources say "August/September 1987"). Sources: Wikipedia Master System; research files.

### Sales Figures

**Claim #14: NES held "eighty-three percent of the North American video game market" by 1988**
- VERIFIED (approximately). The research file cites Infinity Retro as the source for 83%. Some sources cite even higher figures (over 90%). The 83% figure is widely cited and defensible. Sources: Infinity Retro; research files.

**Claim #15: Master System NA: "two million units"**
- VERIFIED. Approximately 2 million units sold in North America. Sources: Wikipedia Master System; VGChartz; research files.

**Claim #16: NES worldwide: "sixty-two million"**
- VERIFIED. The NES sold approximately 61.91 million units worldwide, commonly rounded to 62 million. Sources: Statista; Wikipedia NES; VGChartz.

**Claim #17: Master System: "ten to thirteen million units worldwide"**
- VERIFIED. Standard estimates range from 10 to 13 million units (excluding Tec Toy's Brazilian production). Sources: Wikipedia Master System; VGChartz; research files.

**Claim #18: Master System Europe installed base 1993: "6.25 million" exceeding Mega Drive's "5.73 million"**
- VERIFIED. Wikipedia's Master System article confirms these exact figures for 1993 European installed base. Note: the NES's European installed base was 7.26 million that same year. Sources: Wikipedia Master System.

**Claim #19: France: "1.6 million", UK: "1.35 million"**
- VERIFIED. These figures are confirmed as the Master System's active installed base in those countries in 1993. Sources: Wikipedia Master System.

**Claim #20: Australia: "250,000 units in 1990 alone"**
- VERIFIED. Sources: Wikipedia Master System.

**Claim #21: Brazil Tec Toy: "1989" license, "eight million" variants by 2012**
- VERIFIED. Tec Toy secured the license in 1989 (specifically September 1989), and had sold over 8 million Master System variants by 2012. Sources: Wikipedia Tectoy; Munib Rezaie article.

**Claim #22: Tec Toy "eighty percent of the Brazilian video game market"**
- VERIFIED. Tec Toy claimed 80% of the Brazilian video game market. Sources: Wikipedia Tectoy; research files.

**Claim #23: Combined sales of "150,000 units per year" as late as 2012**
- VERIFIED. By 2012, Tec Toy was still selling approximately 150,000 units per year of combined Master System and Mega Drive variants. Sources: Wikipedia Tectoy; Munib Rezaie article.

### Nintendo Lockout Chip

**Claim #24: 10NES: "two games per year" limit** -- CORRECTION NEEDED
- INCORRECT. The widely documented limit was **five games per year**, not two. Multiple sources confirm the five-game limit, including Wikipedia's NES article, the Atari Games v. Nintendo case documents, and numerous gaming history sources. Companies like Konami famously created subsidiary labels (Ultra Games) to circumvent the five-game limit. The chapter should be corrected from "two" to "five."
- Sources: Wikipedia NES; Video Game Sage forums; Bootleg Games Wiki; ResetEra discussion of Konami/Ultra Games.

**Claim #25: "Nintendo was the sole manufacturer of all cartridges"**
- VERIFIED. Nintendo manufactured all authorized NES cartridges, requiring publishers to pay upfront. Sources: Wikipedia CIC (Nintendo); Medium article on Nintendo Seal of Quality.

**Claim #26: "Cartridges could not be returned"**
- VERIFIED. Publishers bore all inventory risk; cartridges could not be returned to Nintendo. Sources: Wikipedia CIC (Nintendo); research files.

### Sato-Specific Claims

**Claim #29: SG-1000 sold "160,000 units"**
- VERIFIED. The SG-1000 sold 160,000 units in 1983, exceeding Sega's projection of 50,000. Sources: Wikipedia SG-1000; Shmuplations interview; research files.

**Claim #30: "Console Fever" quote**
- VERIFIED against research. Sato's quote: "It was a scale completely incomparable with our arcade board sales. And that is how our entire company caught Console Fever." From the 1998 Famitsu DC interview (Shmuplations translation). Sources: Shmuplations; research files.

**Claim #31: Sato as Mark III "lead"**
- VERIFIED (with nuance). The chapter describes Sato as leading the Mark III development, and research files confirm he "led the design of the custom VDP that was the Mark III's key differentiator." Multiple sources describe him as driving the decision to create custom silicon. Sources: Shmuplations; Sega-16; research files.

**Claim #32: Tom Kalinske: "former Mattel president", hired by SOA "in 1990" (footnote says "mid-1990")**
- VERIFIED. Kalinske was CEO of Mattel from 1985 to 1987 (making "former Mattel president" accurate -- though his exact title was CEO/president). He joined Sega of America in 1990, with some sources specifying November 1990. The chapter text says the hiring was "in 1990" and footnote 58 says "mid-1990" -- the November date would make "late 1990" more accurate than "mid-1990," but this is a minor imprecision. Sources: Wikipedia Tom Kalinske; Sega-16 interview.

### Other Claims

**Claim #33: NES color palette described as "sixteen" colors** -- CORRECTION NEEDED
- INCORRECT. This is the most significant factual error in the chapter. The chapter states: "Its color palette of sixty-four colors was four times larger than the NES's sixteen." This is wrong.

  The NES/Famicom PPU (2C02) has a **total hardware palette of approximately 54 unique colors** (from 64 addressable color values, with some duplicates). It can display **25 colors simultaneously** on screen (13 background + 12 sprite colors). With color emphasis bits, the palette extends to 448 possible color variations.

  The "sixteen" figure appears to be a confusion with the TMS9918A's 16-color fixed palette (used in the SG-1000, not the NES). The NES uses a completely different color generation system.

  **The correct comparison should be:** The Master System's palette of 64 colors compared to the NES's palette of approximately 54 colors -- a modest advantage, not a "four times larger" one. The Master System's advantage was more significant in simultaneous on-screen colors: 32 vs. the NES's 25.

  The chapter text and footnote 27 both need correction.
  Sources: NESdev Wiki PPU palettes; Wikipedia List of video game console palettes; Copetti NES Architecture.

**Claim #34: DX7 synthesizer connection to FM synthesis**
- VERIFIED (with nuance). The YM2413 OPLL and the DX7 both use Yamaha's FM synthesis technology, licensed from John Chowning's Stanford research. However, they are quite different implementations: the DX7 uses the YM21290/YM21280 chips with 6 operators per voice, while the YM2413 is a cost-reduced OPL variant with only 2 operators per channel and mostly fixed instrument patches. The chapter's statement that this was "the same FM synthesis technology" is broadly correct at the conceptual level, though the implementations differ substantially. The connection is fair for a general-audience biography.

**Claim #35: Mega Drive captured "65 percent of the European console market"**
- VERIFIED. Multiple sources confirm the Mega Drive captured 65% of the European console market during the 16-bit era. Sources: Wikipedia Sega Genesis; research files.

**Claim #36: Mega Drive Japan sales: "3.58 million", Super Famicom Japan: "17 million"**
- PARTIALLY VERIFIED. The Mega Drive's 3.58 million Japanese sales figure is confirmed. The Super Famicom sold 17.17 million units in Japan, so "17 million" is a reasonable rounding. However, note that the chapter text (in the closing section) says the NES sold "62 million" worldwide while Sega sold "13 million" -- these are both correct as rough figures. Sources: Wikipedia Sega Genesis; Wikipedia SNES.

**Location:** Paragraph comparing Master System to NES (line ~63, footnote 27)
**Original:** "Its color palette of sixty-four colors was four times larger than the NES's sixteen."
**Corrected:** Revised to accurately describe the NES palette (~54 unique colors from 64 addressable values, 25 simultaneous on-screen) vs. Master System (64-color palette, 32 simultaneous on-screen).

**Original:** "Publishers could release no more than two games per year."
**Corrected:** Changed to "five games per year."

### 3. Footnote 27 (NES Colors)
**Original footnote:** Referenced the NES's palette as "sixteen" colors.
**Corrected footnote:** Revised to accurately describe the NES's approximately 54-color palette and 25 simultaneous on-screen colors vs. the Master System's 64-color palette and 32 simultaneous on-screen colors.

### 4. Footnote 32 (Licensing Limit)

**Corrected footnote:** Changed to "five games per publisher per year."

## Claims Not Requiring Correction (But Worth Noting)

- **Tom Kalinske timing (Claim #32):** Footnote 58 says "mid-1990" but some sources say November 1990. This is a minor imprecision but not clearly wrong -- "mid-1990" could refer to the middle of the calendar year rather than November. Left as-is but flagged for author review.

- **Super Famicom Japan sales (Claim #36):** Chapter says "17 million"; actual figure is 17.17 million. The rounding is reasonable. Left as-is.

- **Mega Drive Europe 5.73 million vs. NES 7.26 million:** The chapter claims the Master System's 6.25 million European installed base "exceeded the Mega Drive's European installed base of 5.73 million." This is correct per the 1993 data. However, the chapter does not mention that the NES's European installed base (7.26 million) was still larger than the Master System's. This is not an error of fact but an omission that could mislead. Flagged for author awareness.
