### 2. Slot machine ban: "US government outlawed slot machines in its territories in 1952"

**Chapter says (footnote 6):** "The U.S. government outlawed slot machines in its territories in 1952."

**Verification:** INCORRECT DATE. The Johnson Act (Transportation of Gambling Devices Act) was enacted on **January 2, 1951**, not 1952. The Act prohibited the transportation of gambling devices (including slot machines) to states where they were illegal, as well as their manufacture, possession, use, sale, or transportation in D.C. and U.S. possessions. The Wikipedia "History of Sega" article says "1952" but this appears to be an imprecise attribution -- the law was passed in 1951 and its enforcement and effects on military bases rolled out over subsequent months. Various Sega history sources round to "1952" because that is when the operational impact was felt (and when Bromley dispatched employees to Japan), but the legislation itself was enacted in 1951.

**Sources:** Johnson Act text (govinfo.gov, 64 Stat. 1134, January 2, 1951); NIGC.gov; Stop Predatory Gambling; NPR (2022) on military slot machines.

**Verdict:** NEEDS CORRECTION. The chapter should either say "1951" (the year the law was enacted) or clarify that while the Johnson Act was passed in 1951, its practical effects prompted Bromley to shift operations overseas by 1952. The current phrasing "in 1952" is misleading.

**Recommended fix:** Change "in 1952" to "in 1951" with a footnote noting the operational impact was felt in 1952.

### 11. Periscope: 1966, first original Sega game, 25-cent play

**Chapter says:** "The first proof of concept came in 1966, when Sega created Periscope, its first original game."

**Chapter says:** "Its success was so pronounced that it prompted operators to raise the standard price of play from ten cents to twenty-five cents."

**Verification:** MOSTLY CONFIRMED, with nuance.

- **1966 date:** Confirmed. Sega released Periscope in Japan in 1966.
- **"First original Sega game":** This is the standard account in most Sega histories. However, Gaming Alexandria and other researchers note that Namco's Masaya Nakamura claimed he built a similar game called "Torpedo Launcher" in 1965. The relationship between the Namco and Sega versions is disputed. The chapter's claim reflects the mainstream historical account.
- **25-cent play:** Confirmed. However, the price change was specifically associated with Sega's 1968 single-player redesign for export, not the 1966 original Japanese release. The chapter implies the 25-cent pricing happened immediately, but it was more of a 1968 development.
- **"First Japanese-made arcade export":** The chapter calls it "the first Japanese-made arcade export," which is confirmed by multiple sources (Wikipedia, Sega Wiki, etc.).

**Verdict:** SUBSTANTIALLY ACCURATE. The 25-cent pricing timeline could be slightly more precise (1968 for the export version, not 1966 for the original Japanese release), but the chapter does not specify a year for the pricing change, so it is not strictly wrong.

### 12. Gulf and Western sale: 1969, "$10 million" for 80% stake

**Chapter says:** "In 1969, Rosen -- by then the sole controlling owner of Sega -- sold the company to Gulf and Western Industries. ... The price was $10 million for the 80 percent stake held by Bromley and Stewart, while LeMaire retained his 20 percent."

**Verification:** The financial details are CONFIRMED. They Create Worlds (citing primary documents) gives the price as $9,977,043 for 80% of stock, between June 1969 and January 1970. LeMaire retained 20%.

**However, there is a logical contradiction in the chapter.** The chapter says both:
1. "Rosen -- by then the sole controlling owner of Sega" (line 65)
2. "The price was $10 million for the 80 percent stake held by Bromley and Stewart" (line 67)

If Rosen was the "sole controlling owner," then Bromley and Stewart could not have sold their 80%. These two statements cannot both be true simultaneously.

**What actually happened:** Multiple Wikipedia sources use the phrase "Rosen, by then the sole owner" but then describe Bromley and Stewart selling 80%. This appears to be an internal contradiction present in the Wikipedia sources themselves. The They Create Worlds blog (which does primary research from corporate documents) indicates that Bromley, Stewart, Rosen, and LeMaire all held shares at the time of the sale. The most likely reading is that Rosen was the de facto operational leader (sole controlling executive) but not the sole owner of shares. The characterization of Rosen as "sole owner" in some sources appears to be inaccurate or imprecise -- he may have been the sole *managing* owner or had acquired majority operational control.

**Verdict:** CONTRADICTION NEEDS CORRECTION. The chapter should not call Rosen "the sole controlling owner" since it goes on to describe Bromley and Stewart selling 80%. The recommended fix is to remove "sole controlling owner" and instead describe Rosen as the chief executive or operational leader.

## Notes (No Correction Required)

### Periscope authorship dispute

The chapter says Periscope was Sega's "first original game" (1966). This is the standard account in mainstream Sega histories. However, Namco's Masaya Nakamura claimed to have built a similar game called "Torpedo Launcher" in 1965. The exact relationship between the two games is disputed among historians. The chapter's claim reflects the accepted mainstream narrative and does not require correction, but this is worth noting for scholarly rigor.

### 25-cent play timing

The chapter says Periscope "prompted operators to raise the standard price of play from ten cents to twenty-five cents." More precisely, the 25-cent pricing was associated with Sega's 1968 single-player export redesign, not the 1966 original. The chapter does not specify which version introduced the price change, so it is not technically wrong, but the implication that it happened in 1966 rather than 1968 is slightly misleading. No correction applied as the chapter does not claim a specific date for the pricing change.

### Revenue figure precision

The $10 million price for the Gulf and Western sale: the They Create Worlds blog, citing primary documents, gives the exact figure as $9,977,043. The chapter's "$10 million" is a standard rounding used in virtually all published accounts.
