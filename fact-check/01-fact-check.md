## Errors Found

### ERROR 1: Founders' Names Swapped (Footnote 67)
- **Claim (footnote 67):** "Service Games was founded in 1940 by Martin Bromberg, Irving Bromley, and James Humpert"
- **ERROR:** The names are transposed. The correct names are **Martin Bromley** and **Irving Bromberg** (not "Martin Bromberg" and "Irving Bromley").
- **Details:** Irving Bromberg was the father; his son was born Martin Jerome Bromberg but went by **Martin Bromley** professionally. Multiple reliable sources (Wikipedia "History of Sega", Kotaku, Abort Retry Fail) consistently list the founders as "Martin Bromley, Irving Bromberg, and James Humpert."
- **Additionally:** The footnote says Service Games was "founded in 1940." In fact, **Standard Games** was founded in May 1940. **Service Games** was established on September 1, **1946**. These are distinct entities. The chapter should either say "Standard Games" for 1940, or "Service Games" for 1946.
- [Sources: Wikipedia "History of Sega"; Kotaku "Meet The Four Americans Who Built Sega"; Abort Retry Fail "A Brief History of Sega Enterprises"]

### ERROR 2: Hirohito's Declaration Described as a "Radio Address"
- **Claim (paragraph 1):** "his divinity had been formally renounced two years earlier in a radio address that most of his subjects could scarcely believe they were hearing"
- **ERROR:** The Humanity Declaration (Ningen-sengen) of January 1, 1946 was an **imperial rescript published in newspapers**, not a radio broadcast. It appeared on the front pages of major newspapers including the Asahi Shimbun and Mainichi Shimbun. The emperor's famous **radio** broadcast was the surrender announcement of August 15, 1945 (the "Jewel Voice Broadcast"), which is a different event. The chapter's text ("radio address that most of his subjects could scarcely believe they were hearing") appears to conflate the two events.
- **Note:** The footnote [^1] correctly identifies the event as the "Declaration of Humanity" delivered "via radio on January 1, 1946" -- but the "via radio" part of the footnote is also incorrect. It was a written rescript in newspapers, not a radio address.
- [Sources: Wikipedia "Humanity Declaration"; Laura Anderman academic paper on Ningensengen]

### ERROR 3: Firebombing Comparison to Atomic Bombs — Needs Qualification
- **Claim:** the firebombing of Tokyo "killed an estimated one hundred thousand people, more than either atomic bomb would individually claim"
- **PARTIALLY INCORRECT / NEEDS QUALIFICATION:** The comparison clearly holds for Nagasaki (60,000-80,000 killed by end of 1945). However, for Hiroshima, estimates of deaths by the end of 1945 range from 90,000 to 140,000, with the most commonly cited Japanese estimate being approximately 140,000. By this measure, Hiroshima's death toll likely **exceeded** the Tokyo firebombing. The claim holds only if comparing **immediate** deaths (first day: 60,000-80,000 at Hiroshima vs. ~100,000 at Tokyo), but the chapter does not specify "immediate." The phrase "would individually claim" implies total casualties, which makes the Hiroshima comparison inaccurate.
- **Recommendation:** Add qualification, e.g., "more than either atomic bomb would immediately claim on the day of the attack" or note that estimates vary and the comparison is most accurate when applied to Nagasaki, or cite the lower-range estimates used by some sources.
- [Sources: Wikipedia "Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki"; Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists; City of Hiroshima official estimates]
